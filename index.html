An overview of effective altruism

intro

Effective altruism is about doing the most good you can. This might be through donating to charities who have demonstrated their effectiveness or it might be through choice of career. It's a loose movement of people guided by evidence rather than their gut.

growth

1971: The seeds were sown by Peter Singer's Famine, Affluence and Morality.

If it is in our power to prevent something bad from happening, without thereby sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance, we ought, morally, to do it.
Taking this idea to it's logical conclusion, Singer suggests that we ought to give until we reach the level of
marginal utilityThe point at which, by giving more, one would experience as much suffering as the beneficiary would avoid
.

The rise of charity evaluators has made it easier to decide where to give based on evidence.

The term effective altruism came in to use around 2010[2].

$1.3 billionBased on members' current age and their own estimates of their future salary
pledged so far[3][4].

Taking a gentler approach, The Life You Can SaveTheir mission: Change the culture of giving in affluent countries while dramatically raising annual donations to highly impactful nonprofits suggests pledging a minimum of 1% of your income, scaling it up for those with larger salaries. Their 18,798 members are estimated to have donated $1.35 million to highly effective charities in 2015.[5]

Making a difference is about more than what you do directly. It's about the things you do which wouldn't have happened otherwise. If you work for in a highly competitive field such as charity work, it's likely that the next best applicant for your jobs will be nearly as good as you. If for instance you work in finance it's unlikely that the next best applicant would give a significant portion of their salary to charities. controversy

Effective altruism is heavily dependant on measurable results, but some potentially high impact causes are very hard to measure.

Not systemic changes / western saviour

Treasure what you can measure - what about things which are harder to measure, but potentially very effective?
